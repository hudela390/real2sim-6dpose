Abstract

Transferring a teleoperated robot demonstration into a physics simulator requires the manipulated object's full per-frame 6-DoF trajectory in the camera frame. Our recordings provide only a single egocentric RGB stream — no metric depth sensor — plus a teleoperation log of the robot and gripper joints. Recovering pose is hard: the objects are near-symmetric (a rubber duck, a circular pan), so appearance alone leaves rotation ambiguous, and the gripper heavily but intermittently occludes the object through grasp, transport and release. We therefore anchor a single high-quality pose and propagate it with a model-based tracker, correcting it with the joint kinematics and the object mask rather than trusting any single per-frame estimate. The result is a per-frame trajectory a simulator can replay, generalizing across scenes and — by swapping only the front-end segmentation — across datasets and object geometries.

Method

A model-based base track, anchored once, then corrected per phase from whichever signal is most trustworthy — partitioned at the ungrasp into an in-hand part and a drop-to-rest part.

1

Front end

Per-frame object mask (SAM2 / SAM3) + a scale-calibrated monocular-depth stream. No depth sensor.

2

Base + anchor

RGBTrack base track, anchored by a frame-0 GoTrack rotation refinement to fix the ambiguous start.

3

In-hand

While gripped: position from the mask, depth from the gripper forward kinematics (h5), rotation from GoTrack as a cleaned noisy teacher.

4

Drop → rest

Once released and unoccluded, full-6-DoF GoTrack tracks the fall; outliers dropped, lightly smoothed.

5

Compose

Concatenate the two parts into the per-frame 4×4 trajectory consumed by the simulation replay.

Egoverse duck

Teleoperated pick-and-place: grasp → carry → drop into the bowl → settle. The subsequent re-grasp out of the bowl is not addressed and is cut.
